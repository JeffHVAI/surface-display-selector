Surface Display Selector ◐

A three-stage process: run the site survey, enter the measured data, and the decision engine derives the required display specification — or tells you projection is the wrong technology and why.

Procedure

S1Define the image and the viewer

Mark the intended display area on the surface with tape. Measure its width and height in metres. Measure the closest distance a user's eyes will normally be from the surface — for an interactive UI this is arm's length (~0.5–0.7 m), not standing-back distance. Record the content class honestly: "video" and "dense interactive UI" produce very different requirements.

S2Measure ambient illuminance (the critical measurement)

What you are measuring: the light already falling on the surface, in lux. This — not room brightness in general — is what the projected image must compete with.

  • Sensor placement: hold the lux meter flat against the display surface, sensor dome facing outward (away from the wall, toward the room). You are measuring light arriving at the surface plane.
  • 5-point pattern: take readings at the four corners and centre of the taped image area. Record all five; the engine uses the worst (highest) point, because a UI must be legible everywhere.
  • Worst time of day: repeat at the brightest realistic condition — midday sun through windows, all lights on, doors open. If you can only visit once, note it and inflate mentally. A reading taken at 7 pm with blinds closed will produce a spec that fails at noon.
  • All lighting states: if lighting is variable (daylight, dimmers, task lights), record min and max. The controllability answer in Tab 2 matters as much as the number.
Calibration check: typical values — dim lounge 50–100 lx · office 300–500 lx · clinical corridor 300–800 lx · retail 750–1500 lx · window-adjacent daylight 2,000–20,000 lx · direct sun 50,000+ lx. If your reading is wildly outside the expected band for the space, re-check sensor orientation.
Common error: pointing the sensor at the light fixture or the window. That measures the source, not the surface. Sensor flat on the surface, facing out.

S3Characterise the surface

Reflectance (colour): hold the white A4 sheet against the surface in even light. Fresh white paper ≈ 0.85 reflectance. Judge the surface against it: indistinguishable → white (0.85); noticeably duller but still light → light (0.60); clearly mid-tone (beige, grey, wood) → 0.35; dark paint, brick, charcoal → 0.15. Select the band in Tab 2.

Specularity (gloss) test: shine the flashlight at the surface at ~45° and look for a mirror-like hotspot from your viewing position. A crisp bright reflection = gloss/semi-gloss (projection will show a glare hotspot and washed contrast at some angles). A soft diffuse glow = matte (good). Record finish.

Texture: run a hand across it. Orange-peel and light texture are fine for video, degrading for small UI text. Heavy texture (brick, stucco) blurs pixel edges — note it.

S4Measure the geometry

With the laser meter, measure the available throw distance: from every feasible mounting position (ceiling point, shelf, above/below the surface) to the centre of the image area. Record the best candidate. Note whether the projector can sit square to the surface (perpendicular, centred or within a lens-shift range) — if it must be skewed, say so in Tab 2; this changes the required optics.

Record whether rear projection is physically possible (access to the far side of glass or a cavity behind the surface), and whether people will pass or stand between any front mounting position and the surface during normal use.

S5Record operating conditions

Daily runtime hours, dust class (office / clinical / industrial / semi-outdoor), acoustic sensitivity (is fan noise a problem at 1 m?), whether the install is permanent or must relocate, and available power/data at each mounting candidate. Photograph everything.

Units note: if your meter reads foot-candles, multiply by 10.76 to get lux before entry.
